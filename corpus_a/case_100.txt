The correct answer is: 2.

Hyperkalemia.

Spironolactone, as a potassium-sparing diuretic, inhibits the action of aldosterone at the renal level, being responsible for a decrease in potassium excretion.

Hyperkalemia produces repolarization disturbances that result in the installation of a large, symmetrical, narrow-based T, visible in the precordial leads.

The QT space is shortened.

These anomalies appear with a kalemia of around 5.5 to 6 mmol/l.

Above 6.5 mmol/L, electrocardiographic changes are constant, and are dominated by conduction disturbances (making ventricular extrasystoles possible).
