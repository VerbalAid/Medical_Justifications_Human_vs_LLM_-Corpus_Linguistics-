The most coherent interpretation of the presented case suggests the presence of Cushing's syndrome (CS). The patient's central obesity, rounded facies, increased supraclavicular fat, and decreased proximal muscle strength, along with easy bruising and arterial hypertension, are consistent with CS. Additionally, the presence of reddish striae in the abdomen and the radiological evidence of osteoporosis and vertebral crushing support this diagnosis.

However, it is crucial to differentiate between exogenous CS (due to excessive use of corticosteroids) and endogenous CS (due to a pituitary or adrenal tumor). To confirm the diagnosis, the patient's urinary free cortisol and basal ACTH levels should be measured. If the basal ACTH is high, it may indicate an adrenal tumor, and an MRI should be performed. If the basal ACTH is low, it may suggest a pituitary micro-adenoma, and a cranial CT scan should be considered.

While type 2 diabetes mellitus and essential hypertension may also be present due to the patient's obesity and age, these conditions alone are not sufficient to explain the patient's signs and symptoms, making Cushing's syndrome the most plausible diagnosis in this case. It is essential to perform the necessary diagnostic tests to confirm the diagnosis and determine the etiology of CS in this patient.
