Given the patient's symptoms, which include sudden and painless unilateral vision loss, and the presence of other systemic issues such as anorexia, weight loss, and musculoskeletal discomfort, an acute ophthalmic condition is suspected. While multiple diagnostic tests could be considered, the initial test requested should be one that can identify potential vascular causes of the vision loss, given the patient's age and systemic issues.

Of the options provided, Magnetic Resonance Angiography (MRA) is the most appropriate first step. MRA is a non-invasive imaging technique that allows for detailed visualization of the arteries and veins within the brain and neck, which includes the carotid arteries. This test is particularly useful in investigating acute ischemic events, such as stroke or transient ischemic attack (TIA), which can cause sudden vision loss.

While lumbar puncture could be considered in the differential diagnosis of meningitis, the absence of symptoms such as headache, photophobia, or neck stiffness argues against this. CRP (C-reactive protein) is a marker of inflammation and may be elevated in various conditions, but it does not specifically identify the cause of the vision loss. Carotid ultrasound could be used to assess carotid stenosis, but it may not provide sufficient detail for the acute onset of symptoms in this case.
