The presented case suggests osteoarthritis (OA) of the knee, given the patient's age, comorbidities, and the clinical findings of joint swelling, varus deformity, limited range of motion, and medial joint line tenderness without a history of trauma. The X-ray findings of osteophytes and mild medial joint space narrowing further support this diagnosis.

Initial management should primarily aim at alleviating the patient's pain and improving mobility. Option 1, which includes paracetamol, metamizole, and naproxen, is a reasonable choice for managing the acute pain. However, the addition of a rescue dose of naproxen, a non-selective COX-2 inhibitor, may increase the risk of gastrointestinal side effects in this patient with comorbidities.

Given the progressive nature of OA, longer-term management should consider non-pharmacological measures such as weight loss, exercise, and physical therapy, in addition to pain management. For severe symptoms, option 2, a referral to a traumatology outpatient clinic for evaluation of a total cemented prosthesis, could be considered as a potential future option, but may be premature at this stage.
