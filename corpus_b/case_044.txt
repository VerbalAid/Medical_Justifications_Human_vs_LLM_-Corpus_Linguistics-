The presented case suggests premature thelarche, a condition characterized by precocious breast development in the absence of other secondary sexual characteristics, such as pubic or axillary hair, in girls under the age of 8. The normal external genitalia, small uterus on ultrasonography, and bone age matching the chronological age further support this diagnosis.

While it is essential to consider alternative conditions that may cause precocious puberty, such as gonadotropin-secreting tumors, central precocious puberty, or McCune-Albright syndrome, given the absence of other signs typically associated with these conditions, they seem less likely in this case.

Given the benign nature and self-limiting course of premature thelarche, a follow-up every 3-4 months (option 1) would be an appropriate approach, as this condition often resolves on its own. Breast biopsy (option 2) and mammography (option 3) are unnecessary and may cause anxiety without providing diagnostic value in this context. Administration of GnRH analogues (option 4) is typically used to suppress premature puberty in central precocious puberty, which is different from the presented case.

Although the possibility of other etiologies cannot be completely ruled out, the current presentation is most consistent with premature thelarche, and a watchful waiting approach with regular follow-ups seems appropriate, avoiding invasive and unnecessary investigations.
