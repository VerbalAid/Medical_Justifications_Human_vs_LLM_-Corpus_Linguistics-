The suspected diagnosis is likely motor neuron disease, given the progressive and asymmetric limb weakness with distal predominance and fasciculations. The diagnostic test that would confirm this is an Electromyographic (EMG) study, which can help identify the characteristic motor neuron abnormalities in this condition. Although a Cerebral Magnetic Resonance Imaging (MRI) might reveal atrophy in affected regions, it is less specific than EMG for motor neuron disease. A Cerebral Computed Tomography (CT) is not typically helpful in this context, and Multimodal evoked potentials are used more for the assessment of brainstem and spinal cord function rather than motor neuron disease.
