Based on the presented clinical case, the most plausible diagnosis appears to be Autoimmune Hemolytic Anemia (AIHA). The patient's history of high fever and dry cough suggests a concurrent infection, which could have triggered an immune response leading to AIHA. The laboratory findings support this diagnosis:

1. Leukocytosis (16900 leukocytes/mm3) and thrombocytosis (360000 platelets/mm3) are commonly seen in reactive conditions, such as infections and autoimmune disorders.

2. The marked anisocytosis, macrocytosis (MCV 109 fl), and polychromatophilia indicate anemia with varying maturity of red blood cells, which is consistent with AIHA.

3. The presence of spherocytic forms could suggest a hemolytic anemia, although in this case, it is more likely a result of AIHA rather than hereditary spherocytosis (Hereditary Spherocytosis, option 2), as the patient's history does not suggest a familial predisposition and there are no other characteristic features of Hereditary Spherocytosis, such as splenomegaly or positive family history.

4. The raised unconjugated bilirubin (6.1 mg/dl) and LDH (2408 U/L) levels indicate hemolysis, a key feature of AIHA. The high LDH level, especially when compared to normal GOT and GPT, suggests extravascular hemolysis, which is characteristic of AIHA.

5. The positive panagglutinin in the irregular antibody study, making crossmatching difficult, further supports the diagnosis of AIHA. This finding indicates the presence of multiple antibodies against red blood cells, which is a common feature of AIHA.

6. While Acute Leukemia (option 5) could present with similar symptoms and laboratory findings, the absence of blasts on the morphological study and the lack of other features such as bone marrow involvement or peripheral blasts argues against this diagnosis in this case.
