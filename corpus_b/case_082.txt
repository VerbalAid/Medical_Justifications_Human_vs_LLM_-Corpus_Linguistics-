The presented case describes a cryptorchidism, where the undescended testicle remains in the inguinal canal instead of descending into the scrotum. This condition is commonly identified at birth or in the early neonatal period, but this case demonstrates a late presentation. The most likely diagnosis is congenital (primary) cryptorchidism, which accounts for about 80-90% of all cases. This is characterized by failure of the testicle to descend into the scrotum during fetal development.
