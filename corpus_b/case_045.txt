The presented case describes a 40-year-old woman experiencing recurrent, brief episodes of intense left periocular pain accompanied by tearing and rhinorrhea. Given the absence of any neurological deficits or abnormalities on examination or MRI, the most likely diagnosis for this symptomatology is Cluster Headache (CH).

Cluster Headache is a primary headache disorder characterized by severe, unilateral periorbital pain, autonomic symptoms such as tearing and rhinorrhea, and a periodic course. It predominantly affects males, but the female-to-male ratio is estimated to be 1:1–3 in some studies. The typical episodic cluster period lasts from several weeks to a few months, with remissions of varying duration. The nocturnal onset and the predominance of attacks during sleep are also characteristic features of CH.

Among the provided treatment options, Indomethacin is considered the first-line treatment for CH. It is a nonsteroidal anti-inflammatory drug (NSAID) with potent analgesic properties, particularly effective in alleviating the intense pain associated with CH. Other pharmacological interventions for CH include verapamil, lithium carbonate, and corticosteroids like prednisone. However, these agents are typically considered second-line or adjunctive treatments due to their less favourable side-effect profiles compared to Indomethacin. Lamotrigine, an anticonvulsant medication, is not a conventional treatment for cluster headaches and is not indicated in this case.
