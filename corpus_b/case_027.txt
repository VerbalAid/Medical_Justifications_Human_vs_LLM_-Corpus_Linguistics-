Given the 6-day fever evolution, conjunctival injection, rash that confluences but not scarlatiniform, rash on the trunk and genital area, erythema with edema in hands and feet, and unilateral cervical lymphadenopathy, the most likely clinical diagnosis is Kawasaki disease, particularly due to the absence of typical measles or scarlet fever symptoms (rash with a fine, sandpaper-like texture and a strawberry tongue) and rubella's characteristic rash spreading from the trunk to the extremities. However, it's essential to consider these other conditions in the differential diagnosis, especially in the context of a febrile child presenting with a rash.
