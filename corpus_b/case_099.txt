In Alzheimer's disease (AD), symptoms typically progress in a specific order, with cognitive deficits being an early and prominent feature. The sequence of symptoms is most closely aligned with option 3: Mood changes, cognitive deficit, decline of functional independence.

While behavioral symptoms and memory loss are both characteristic of AD, mood changes and cognitive decline often precede noticeable behavioral changes or motor symptoms. Mood changes, such as depression, are usually observed in the later stages of the disease, as the individual struggles with the cognitive and functional decline.

Functional decline is a later manifestation of AD, as the patient finds it increasingly difficult to carry out daily activities due to the progressive cognitive deterioration.

Although the sequence of symptoms in AD can vary among individuals, the most common pattern involves cognitive deficits, mood changes, and functional decline, in that order.
