Based on the clinical presentation and imaging findings, a diagnosis of acute ischemic stroke secondary to large vessel occlusion, likely in the middle cerebral artery territory, is the most probable diagnosis. The patient's symptoms of headache, fever, and aphasia, along with the radiological evidence of a hypodensity in the left temporal lobe without mass effect or contrast uptake, are consistent with an ischemic stroke. Although meningitis may present similarly, it typically causes nuchal rigidity and would be less likely in the absence of a CSF examination. Limbic encephalitis usually presents with memory disturbances, psychiatric symptoms, and seizures, which are not prominent in this case. Brain abscess is less likely given the lack of contrast enhancement and the absence of symptoms like seizures or focal neurological deficits other than aphasia. However, further evaluation with MRI and CSF examination would be necessary
