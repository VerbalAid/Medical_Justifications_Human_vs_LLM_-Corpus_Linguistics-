The patient's markedly low calcium level and elevated PTH suggest primary hyperparathyroidism (PHPT). The family history further supports this diagnosis. Although 1,25-OH 2D (vitamin D) and PTHrP can be elevated in PHPT, they are more indicative of secondary hyperparathyroidism in the setting of vitamin D deficiency or other causes, respectively. 25-OH D is a measure of vitamin D stores and is less relevant in this case. Urine calcium/creatinine ratio and phosphate tubular reabsorption are not specific for PHPT and are not the most useful tests in confirming the diagnosis.
