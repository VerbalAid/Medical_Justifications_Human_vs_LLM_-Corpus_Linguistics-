The diagnosis most likely supported by the vignette is Q Fever, caused by the bacterium Coxiella burnetii. This entity is transmitted through inhalation of contaminated particles from the feces or urine of infected animals, such as sheep, goats, and cattle, as well as wild animals.

In the acute form, Q Fever typically presents with fever, headache, myalgia, and non-productive cough, similar to the patient's symptoms. Chest pain and bilateral pulmonary infiltrates on chest X-ray are common findings in the pulmonary phase of the disease.

Serologic testing, such as the one performed in this case, often reveals elevated antibodies against phase II antigens of Coxiella burnetii, supporting a diagnosis of Q Fever. The patient does not generally have elevated antibodies to phase I antigens, as suggested in option 3.
