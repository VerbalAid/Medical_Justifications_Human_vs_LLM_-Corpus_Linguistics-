Based on the presented symptoms of polyuria and polydipsia, as well as the laboratory findings of hypernatremia (148 mEq/L) and hyperosmolality (309 mOsm/kg) with a low urinary osmolality (89 mOsmlkg), the patient likely has diabetes insipidus, either central or nephrogenic.

In this context, the next diagnostic test should be aimed at confirming the absence or presence of antidiuretic hormone (ADH) in the patient. Since the plasma osmolality and natremia are elevated, and urinary osmolality is low, it suggests a deficiency in the body's ability to respond to ADH, indicative of nephrogenic diabetes insipidus.
