In this clinical scenario, a 47-year-old man with a history of cataract surgery and myopia magna suddenly experiences a profound, painless loss of vision in his right eye. This symptomatology suggests an intraocular process, and the most likely diagnosis among the provided options is **Post-surgical endophthalmitis (1)**.

Post-surgical endophthalmitis is a rare but serious complication of ocular surgery, characterized by inflammation and infection of the inner layers of the eye. Symptoms typically present within a few days to a week post-surgery, with rapid vision loss being a key feature. The patient's history of cataract surgery and the acute onset of vision loss strongly support this diagnosis.

While other possibilities such as retinal detachment (2) and posterior vitreous detachment (4) could cause vision loss, they are less likely in this context. Retinal detachment usually presents with symptoms like flashing lights, floaters, or peripheral vision loss, which are not reported in this case. Posterior vitreous detachment is a common age-related change that usually does not cause a sudden, profound loss of vision.

Age-related macular degeneration, wet form (3), typically presents with central vision loss, which is often gradual, not sudden. Additionally, the patient's history of cataract surgery makes it less likely for wet AMD to be the primary cause of his symptoms.
