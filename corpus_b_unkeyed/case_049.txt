The most likely diagnosis in this case appears to be Hyper IgE syndrome (HIES, option 2). This rare primary immunodeficiency disorder is characterized by elevated IgE levels, recurrent bacterial and viral infections, and cutaneous abnormalities, including atopic dermatitis, which is consistent with the patient's history.

Recurrent infections affecting different sites, such as otitis media, meningitis, and pneumonia, support the diagnosis. Although Wiskott-Aldrich syndrome (option 1) can also present with recurrent infections, it is typically characterized by thrombocytopenia, eczema, and immunological dysfunction, which may not be as pronounced in this case.

X-linked severe combined immunodeficiency (SCID, option 4) is another possibility, but it usually presents with severe and early-onset infections, often before the age of 6 months, and lacks the characteristic IgE elevation seen in HIES. Transient hypogammaglobulinemia of childhood (option 3) is a self-limiting condition that resolves by 5 years of age, and common variable immunodeficiency (option 5) typically presents with a more variable and less pronounced immunological profile.

In this case, the combination of recurrent infections, cutaneous abnormalities, and specific immunological findings, including decreased T-lymphocyte subpopulations, elevated IgA and IgE, decreased IgM and IgG, support a diagnosis of Hyper IgE syndrome. Further confirmatory tests, such as analysis of STAT3 gene mutations, would be required for definitive diagnosis.
